AutoZone: auto parts and accessories

Autoparts June 10, 2022 0 6 min read

Hello all,

I wanted to share with you my appreciation of AutoZone.

It was thanks to Philippe’s newsletters that I became interested in AutoZone.
NL#119 on companies massively buying back their shares, and NL#215 The most profitable big caps on the planet.

AutoZone is the leading US distributor ($11 billion turnover) of automotive spare parts and accessories with more than 5,000 stores in the United States, Puerto Rico, Mexico and Brazil. Each store has an extensive line for cars, sport utility vehicles, vans and light trucks, including new and reconditioned hard parts, maintenance items and accessories.
The first store opened Forrest City, Arkansas on July 4, 1979 as the Auto Shack. The company changed its name in 1987.
The company also distributes parts and accessories on its website and on an Apple and Android app.
They also offer automotive diagnostic and repair software (external growth in 1996).

AutoZone opens an average of 180 stores each year and operates without a franchise (35 openings in the USA in Q3 of the current fiscal year + 8 in Mexico, 205 net openings in 2016).
In Q3, its 5,889 stores are distributed as follows:
US: 5381
Mexico: 499 (implant since 1998)
Brazil: 9 (since 2012)
They own the land and walls, which they resell or sublet to other types of businesses when they want to close/move the store.

AutoZone wrote:

In 1996, AutoZone launched a commercial program offering credit and delivery to professional technicians.

I admit that I didn’t really understand what this activity is, it still represents $1 billion in turnover in 2011.

EDIT:
An article on SeekingAlpha made it clear to me:

SeekingAlpha translated by Google wrote:

In addition, many stores have a trade sales program that offers trade credit and fast delivery of parts and other products to local, regional and national repair garages, dealerships, service stations and accounts. of the public sector.

In 2012 AutoZone acquired AutoAnything, an online retailer of specialty automotive products.

Evolution of sales since 1994

Growth slowed sharply as a critical size is reached, but the international market is only at the beginning and the US is still growing slightly.

Gross and Operating Margin (EBIT) constantly increasing since 1993 (Price Power)

EPS evolution since 2002 (long live the share buybacks, of course)

For the number of securities, I had the choice depending on the data source between, 28.16 (WSJ presentation page), (28.41 FT), 29.0 (AZO Q3 2017), 29.3 (AZO cumulative Q3 2017) and 30 (xlsvaluation 2016 therefore WSJ Income Statement).

This is the problem with companies massively buying back their shares, you never know how many there are! I took 29.30 for safety (the more the more it’s dilutive) but I think the fairest would have been 29.0. The spread is small and only affects the valuation by $4.

Superb fundamentals

Solvency ratios that seem quite correct to me (net debt 2 times EBITDA (Kellogg’s is 3.5) and very high coverage of financial charges)

Very high payout ratios (too?). No dividends, only share buybacks, which is great for capitalization and (personal) reinvestment in the business.

Does this mean that the company is going into debt to buy back its own shares?
I think so, it is a risky practice in the event of a downturn in activity.

However today, given the contained debt AMHJ (2 x EBITDA), the magnitude of the ROIC (greater than 40%) and the average cost of capital below 8% including the low cost of debt (3.1% gross, 2.0% net ) I think it’s very accretive.

Profitability ratios

Gross margin and MOP are consistent between xlsValorisation and AZO’s presentations.

FCF margins and profit margins are correlated: a sign of the “reliability” of the net result.
The ROE is negative and ineffective, normal for a company massively buying back its shares.
ROIC & CROIC are excellent even if the trend is constrained.
Note that AZO estimates its ROIC at around 32%. I didn’t have fun digging into the calculation, but as with Nike, we note this difference in method.

It should also be noted that the cash position is still capped at $200 million. In my opinion, this is a sign that it is well used and invested in profitability between new store openings and share buybacks ($1.2 billion/year on average over the last 5 years).

A reflection on historical PERs below:

The lowest PER was 12.8 in 2009 (financial crisis), otherwise 13.6 in 2007. The highest 20 in 2015.

Let me quote a sentence from the book Devenir Rentier by Philippe:

HI wrote:

Cyclical companies alternate between large and small profits depending on economic cycles. It is at the top of the cycle that their profits are the highest and the PER the most
weak ; and at the bottom of the cycle that their profits are the lowest and therefore the PER the highest. Thus, for cyclical companies, often the lower the PER, the less time to buy!

Are auto parts retailers as cyclical as car manufacturers? This is not reflected in AZO’s figures, even in the long term (since 1990).
I don’t really know what to think about it, the current PER of 14 TTM would imply the end of the cycle, but the highest (beginning of the cycle?) was barely 2 years ago.

This PER TTM is mainly linked AMHA the sharp fall in the price since January (-25% the ladle).

Itself is linked to rather good results (in my opinion) but less good overall. Same at the end of 2016.
Which made me think of Stock Market Newsletter #141 – Beware of daily media and financial noise

In short, I decided not to take it into account since my interpretation of this news is more positive than the actors of the “market”.

For the EPS xlsValorisation takes the net result N-1 divided by the current number of shares.
According to the sources I found the following estimates:

At the discretion of each

The turnover is the cumulative 2017 / 36 x 52 (method difference from EPS but result more conservative than other methods)
The EBIT + rate is the cumulative 2017 (lower than the estimate of xlsValorisation) as well as the tax rate and the debt.

The difference between the NOPAT and the Net Income of the presentation is that the NOPAT does not take interest expense into account.

Discount rates

As usual I prefer historical rates.
For the beta I used the “Retail Automotive” industry, because the beta of the “Auto Loans” industry seemed to me very high (1.37) compared to that of the company.
I took the highest between Damodaran and that of the company.
I could have taken Auto Parts and let xlsValorisation take the average of the two models, we would have a beta of 0.99, which is more favorable than my estimate.

Growth in operating income

Who gives us the valuation

The valuation reveals a high uncertainty (in other words the beta is greater than 1).
Damodaran’s beta is based on the company’s industry and debt burden, but given the company’s historical beta, this uncertainty is AMHA’s perspective.
If we use the average of the 2 betas (as does xlsValorisation d’origine) with the beta of the Auto Parts industry, i.e. an average of 0.99, the uncertainty becomes normal and the recommended price to buy is only -7% compared to yesterday, the intrinsic value ($705) estimates a safety margin of 16% and an upside objective of 27%.
